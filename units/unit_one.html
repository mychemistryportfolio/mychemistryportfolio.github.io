Criteria: “I can describe the components of an atom and discuss the experimental evidence and theory that supports their existence, charge and location.”

Course Notes

Throughout my academic career, I have found it quite useful to write down my notes on
paper as it helps me remember information later. I find it imperative to write down anything important
rather than typing it up so I can remember the information for a longer period. I’ve submitted my notes
for properties of atoms as evidence. While writing these notes, I often take a moment to reread what I
have written to ensure that I understand the lesson. The Greeks, Rutherford, Dalton, Thomson, Nagaoka,
Bohr, and Chadwick were responsible for creating the foundations of modern chemistry. When studying
for an assessment, I will go back to reread my notes and refresh my understanding of the relevant topics.


Criteria: “I can describe the number and location of orbitals and electrons using quantum numbers, orbital diagrams and electron configurations.”

POGIL

The Quantum Theory is an important theory in modern day chemistry. It can be overwhelming to try and
remember all the rules regarding the four quantum numbers. I made a note to practice questions regarding
quantum numbers, and the various concepts related to them. As evidence I have submitted a POGIL Worksheet
that I completed. It was an interesting worksheet that explained quantum numbers, orbital diagrams, and
electron configurations. I found it helpful especially because it didn’t use any complex terminology
and the scenario of a boarding house made it easier to understand electron configurations.


Criteria: “I can determine the geometric arrangement, shape, bond angles and polarity of a molecule by drawing a proper Lewis diagram and using VSEPR theory.”

VSEPR Simulation Website

Sometimes, a visual representation or model sits better in my head. To better understand the
VSEPR Theory, bonding angles, geometric arrangement, shape, and polarity, I found an online simulation
website that provided the major details of a molecule when provided the number of bonds and lone pairs.
Using this website allowed me to deepen my understanding of the VSEPR Theory and how to name molecules
based on their given properties.


Criteria: “I can determine the difference between atomic, molecular (both polar and non-polar), ionic, metallic and network solids, and predict their properties based on the structure’s intermolecular forces.”

Discussion

I am a strong advocate for class discussions. Often, students can teach key concepts in a much more
understandable method. The lesson regarding atomic, molecular, ionic, metallic, and network solids
challenged some of my friends. There were some characteristics of the solids that made it hard to
differentiate it from other solids. As evidence, I have attached a discussion I had with some friends
who are also taking grade 12 chemistry. There was confusion regarding the classification of different
molecules. By the end of the discussion, my friends and I had a much stronger understanding of the
properties prior to the lab that we were to complete the next day.

Properties of Solids Lab

Labs are an excellent method to try and apply what you have learned in the real world. The hands-on
experience is beneficial to students and strengthens their understanding. The evidence I have submitted
is the “Properties of Solids” lab that my class completed at the end of the unit. Applying the knowledge
I learned in class allowed me to experience what it truly feels like to determine the properties of solids
using a variety of methods and tools. The lab also served as a good experiment prior to the unit test.
